## Five activities to try first

These fit a 50-min Starfish TPPP lesson (warm-up → diagnostic → presentation →
guided practice → production → wrap-up) and need no prep beyond dropping photos
into `images-*/`.

1. **Schema-activation warm-up** *(any age, 5 min)* — Fully blurred portrait.
   Drop one window. Students hypothesize in L2 ("I think he is…", "He might be a
   doctor"). Reveal one more window per turn. Don't confirm or reject — let the
   discussion ride.
2. **Eiken Pre-1 narration prep** *(High School, B1+)* — Four images that tell a
   story. Reveal one at a time, out of order. After each reveal, students
   narrate what came before and predict what comes next. Replay in correct
   order at the end to check.
3. **Present continuous / prepositions drill** *(Elementary–JHS, A1–A2)* — Photo
   of a busy scene (kitchen, market). Drop four to six small windows in
   different spots; each window elicits one present-continuous sentence ("She
   is cutting carrots").
4. **Modal-of-deduction practice** *(JHS–HS, A2–B1)* — Photo with ambiguous
   clues (a person, a half-open door, a dropped object). Reveal progressively;
   students move from "might be" to "must be" as more windows appear.
5. **Story reconstruction** *(HS–Adult, B1+)* — Five to six photos from a real
   event. Reveal in non-chronological order. Students describe each, then
   re-order and narrate the full story in past tenses.

## Activity catalog (from ELT Activity Library)

Source: NotebookLM notebook `52a64516` (ELT Activity Library). Most entries are
named classic ELT activities; the tool is the digital equivalent of the
paper-and-cardboard versions.

| Activity | Setup (tool action) | Level / Objective | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Slow Reveal** | Start fully blurred; drop one window per turn. | Any / speaking, speculation, vocab | Don't confirm/reject guesses — let discussion ride. |
| **Windows Reveal** *(literal cardboard prototype)* | Drop several windows on one photo, one at a time. | Speaking, listening, vocab | The tool's exact digital analogue. |
| **Strip of a Magazine Picture** | One tall thin spotlight down the centre of a busy photo. | Vocab guessing, negotiation | Pre-cut the image to a 5mm–1cm strip before class for fastest use. |
| **Flashing a Picture** | Drop a window briefly; close it; drop again elsewhere. | Memory, detail recall | Start very fast, then slow down. |
| **Building Up a Picture** | Four deliberate reveals: face → body → setting → action. | Elem–Int / speaking, vocab | Four stages keeps momentum. |
| **Spot the Difference** | Two photos side by side, revealed at different paces. | Beg–Adv / speaking, prepositions, present continuous | Forbid students from marking the actual image — use separate notes. |
| **Story Reconstruction (Visual Jigsaw)** | Five to six sequence photos, revealed out of order. | Int–Adv / narrative tenses | Some ambiguity in sequence → better discussion. |
| **Memory Pictures** *(Would You Make a Good Witness?)* | Full image for 20–60 sec → full blur → recall quiz. | Vocab, past continuous | Frame as a playful "are you reliable?" challenge. |
| **Silhouettes** | Heavy blur that preserves only shapes. | Present progressive, interrogatives | Image needs strong silhouettes to read. |
| **What's the Link?** | Two different images, revealed in parallel. | Discussion, reasoning | Pairs well with adult conversation classes. |

`Describe and Draw` (one student describes, the other draws without seeing the
image) is a near-fit but needs a student-facing tool — analog fallback is to
hand the student a printout and a sheet of paper.

## Level placement (CEFR + Eiken)

Source: CEFR Companion Volume (2020) + Eiken grade descriptors, via notebook
`86d27c3c`.

| CEFR / Eiken | Suitable activities |
|---|---|
| **Pre-A1 / A1** | Silhouettes, Memory Pictures, Slow Reveal (vocab) |
| **A2** | Slow Reveal (description), Building Up a Picture, Spot the Difference |
| **B1** | Story Reconstruction, Flashing, Windows Reveal (full description) |
| **B2** | What's the Link, multi-image narrative, B1 report-style with photos |
| **Eiken Grade 4–5** | Slow Reveal of a scene — answer the examiner's questions about it |
| **Eiken Grade 3** | Windows Reveal of one photo — picture-card description |
| **Eiken Pre-2 / 2** | One portrait + reveal — "What is the person thinking or intending to do?" |
| **Eiken Pre-1** | Four reveals in sequence — 4-panel cartoon narration |

At A1, the CEFR explicitly says understanding is dependent on delivery being
"accompanied by visuals or manual gestures." At B2, learners are expected to
"make abstract concepts accessible by visually representing them" — pairs the
reveal with a follow-up mind-map or flow-chart activity.

## SLA grounding (Ellis)

Source: Rod Ellis, notebook `b5c4069c`.

- **Information gap required.** If students just label what's visible, it's a
  drill, not a task. The reveal must create a gap that *requires* language to
  bridge.
- **Hidden focus on form.** Pick activities where the target form *emerges*
  ("going to", modals of deduction, past continuous) without being announced.
  Picture tasks are well-suited to this — the form is "hidden" in the scene.
- **Visuals as comprehensible input.** Visuals provide the extra-linguistic
  context that makes $i+1$ language processable. Beginners in particular
  benefit from a silent-receptive phase before any production is required.
- ⚠️ **Processing-load caveat.** Requiring a learner to describe a complex
  scene while a window is actively moving *increases* processing load and can
  harm fluency or accuracy. Reveal → pause → describe.

## Design best practices (Mayer / LXD)

Source: Mayer (cognitive load + multimedia), Dirksen (motivation), Clark (LXD)
via notebook `2f82f2fb`.

**Do:**
- Reveal in roughly **four stages** to keep momentum.
- **Model the activity first** with a student volunteer at the front.
- Provide **marker words and prepositions** for A1–A2 before starting
  ("top-right", "in the corner", "behind").
- **Pause** after each reveal before asking students to respond.
- Choose images with **consequence** — a scene where a story could unfold, not
  a stock photo. (Dirksen: the Elephant checks out if the picture feels
  trivial.)
- **Reveal one image, then prompt**: "What do you see? What might happen next?
  What did you notice first?" before revealing more.

**Don't:**
- **Narrate what students can already see.** Mayer: that's the redundancy
  effect — it overloads the verbal + visual channels.
- **Move or close windows faster than working memory can absorb.** Information
  that never makes it past working memory is never encoded.
- **Use the tool with advanced learners who already have schemas for the whole
  image** — the spotlight becomes busywork. Add task complexity (e.g., "justify
  your hypothesis using two details") rather than visual gating.
- **Frame as a "puzzle-without-stakes."** If the picture feels trivial, the
  activity feels trivial. Choose photos with human-scale stories.

Patterns documented in the curated CELTA material:

- **Schema activation with portraits.** Open with a blurred portrait of a
  person; elicit "Who is he? Is he married? What did he do?" before any grammar
  is taught. Sets up the lesson's context before any form appears.
- **"Slightly obscured" photograph.** Show a clear version first to elicit a
  target sentence, then switch to a partially obscured version — students
  reconstruct the sentence from limited visual clues. Memory and prediction
  together.
- **Vocabulary recycling with photos.** Use a single photo to elicit and
  recycle a lexical set across several turns (e.g., health vocabulary: *pain*,
  *tired*, *dizzy*, *fever*).
- **Evidence-based prediction.** Show a photo of an action about to happen (a
  man about to kick a cat); elicit "going to" from present visual evidence.
- **The Jo filter for picture tasks.** Every picture task needs (a) a clear
  language aim, (b) meaningful context (not "sentences in isolation"), (c) real
  cognitive processing, and (d) **psychological authenticity** — students have
  a real reason to listen to each other, not just repeat a structure.

## Framing for parents and adult learners

Source: Patrick Sherriff, notebook `f391d437`.

Patrick's notebook does not directly discuss image-reveal — he describes his
teaching style as "very analog" (whiteboards, paper, flashcards). His
applicable philosophy:

- **"Show more, tell less."** The tool forces students to *use English* to
  hypothesize about what they see. That hypothesising is the lesson, not the
  reveal.
- **To parents:** market the activity as a **confidence-and-independence
  builder** — a "brain-training" exercise that removes the fear of being wrong
  by giving students something concrete to react to.
- **To adult learners who "just want conversation":** use the tool as a
  **kicking-off point** — solves the silence problem when students run out of
  things to say.
- **Watch out:** tech can become a "crutch" that takes away from the human
  contact students pay for. The tool is justified only if it gets students
  *talking more* than the teacher.
